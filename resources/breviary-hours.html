The Hours of the Roman Breviary

Restoring traditional understanding of the Divine Office

Over time, the Breviary’s canonical hours have been misaligned from their original solar-temporal structure. This table compares the common modern assumptions with the traditional, monastic and theological understanding rooted in the Rule of St. Benedict, the early Fathers, and devotional interpretation of Christ’s Passion.

Modern Misconception Traditional Understanding
Matins: A midnight office, rarely recited outside monasteries. Matins: Often recited during the First Watch of the night, often soon after sunset by conventual monasteries. Most observant monasteries might observe it during the Second or Third Watch. Sometimes prayed earlier in the afternoon. Traditionally structured in three Nocturns, each linked to one of the first three vigils. Devotionally, it marks Christ’s agony in the garden and arrest.
Lauds: The morning office, usually prayed at sunrise or upon rising. Lauds: Associated with cockcrow, the turning point into the Fourth Watch. Often paired with Matins. Devotionally linked to Christ’s trial before the Sanhedrin and Peter’s denial.
Prime: Historically placed at 6:00 AM. Often seen as redundant alongside Lauds. Prime: Marks the first hour after sunrise. Devotionally, recalls Christ judged by Pilate.
Terce: A mid-morning prayer, around 9:00 AM. Terce: Third solar hour after sunrise, ending near mid-morning. Marks the sentencing and Christ’s journey to Calvary; associated with market opening.
Sext: The midday office, typically at 12:00 PM. Sext: Sixth and final ante meridiem hora, concluding before solar noon. Marks Christ’s Crucifixion.
None: Prayed at 3:00 PM, often called the Hour of Mercy. None: Third post meridiem hora, soon after noon. Marks the death of Christ. Traditionally occurred earlier than 3 PM.
Vespers: Prayed in the evening, often after dark. Vespers: Late afternoon, before sunset. Followed by a light meal. Devotionally reflects the removal of Christ’s body from the Cross.
Compline: The final night prayer, often before bed. Compline: A pre-sunset “stocktake” prior to retiring. Marks Christ’s burial and the sealing of the tomb.

These ancient hours were not defined by mechanical clocks but by the heavens themselves. The Roman Solar Time app offers a renewed opportunity to align life, work, and prayer to the natural rhythms once honoured by saints and monks alike.
