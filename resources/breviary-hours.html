The Hours of the Roman Breviary

Restoring traditional understanding of the Divine Office

Over time, the Breviary’s canonical hours have been misaligned from their original solar-temporal structure. This table compares the common modern assumptions with the traditional, monastic and theological understanding rooted in the Rule of St. Benedict, the early Fathers, and devotional interpretation of Christ’s Passion.

Modern Misconception Traditional Understanding
Matins: A midnight office, rarely recited outside monasteries. Matins: Often recited during the First Watch of the night, often soon after sunset by conventual monasteries. Most observant monasteries might observe it during the Second or even the Third Watch. Sometimes prayed earlier in the afternoon by monastic communities. Traditionally structured in three Nocturns, each nocturn being associated with the first three night watches. Devotionally, it marks Christ’s agony in the garden and arrest.
Lauds: The morning office, usually prayed at sunrise or upon rising. However, some secular clergy may delay it to the mid-morning. Lauds: Associated with cockcrow, the turning point between the third and fourth watch. While more observant monastic communities did observe it during the Fourth Watch, the more Conventual would combine it with Matins, which often meant that it, too, was frequently anticipated the previous afternoon. Devotionally linked to Christ’s trial before the Sanhedrin and Peter’s denial.
Prime: Historically placed at 6:00 AM. However, since Lauds is already understood to be the office of sunrise, it was suppressed in recent times. Prime: Marks the first hour after sunrise, commencing the monastic workday. Devotionally, recalls Christ’s appearance before Pontius Pilate.
Terce: A mid-morning prayer, around 9:00 AM. Terce: The third solar hour after sunrise. Ends near mid-morning. Devotionally, it marks the moment Christ was sentenced and began His journey to Calvary. Traditionally associated with the hour at which the market day commenced.
Sext: The midday office, typically begun at 12:00 PM. Sext: The sixth and final ante meridiem hora, concluding just before solar noon. Devotionally associated with the Crucifixion, which began at this hour.
None: Prayed at 3:00 PM, often called the Hour of Mercy. None: The third post meridiem hora, ending relatively soon after noon. Marks the hour when Christ died. While the modern “3 PM Hour of Mercy” is popular, the Scriptural time of Christ's death would likely have been earlier, especially since Good Friday was regarded to have occurred near the equinox.
Vespers: Prayed in the evening, often after dark. Vespers: Recited in the late afternoon, before sunset, anticipating the end of labour and the arrival of eventide. Separated from Compline by a light repast. Devotionally reflects the taking down of Christ’s body and the quieting of the day.
Compline: The final night prayer, often said before bed. Compline: A pre-sunset “stocktake” to conclude communal activity, followed by a light repast and retiring. Devotionally, associated with Christ’s burial and the sealing of the tomb.

These ancient hours were not defined by mechanical clocks but by the heavens themselves. The Roman Solar Time app offers a renewed opportunity to align life, work, and prayer to the natural rhythms once honoured by saints and monks alike.
